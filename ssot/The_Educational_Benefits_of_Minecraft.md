Introduction 

Minecraft has evolved beyond entertainment, becoming a versatile tool in 

educational settings. Minecraft Education, the version used for educational 

purposes, integrates gaming with learning, offering an immersive environment 

that enhances various educational outcomes. 

This paper explores how Minecraft can be used across different subjects beyond 

mathematics, fostering skills such as creativity, collaboration, critical thinking, and 

problem-solving. 

Minecraft as an Educational Tool 

Minecraft's open-ended nature allows for its application in diverse educational 

scenarios, ranging from science and technology to language arts and history. The 

game's environment, where players create and manipulate a world made of 

blocks, serves as a virtual classroom where educational concepts can be visualized 

and experienced directly through applied learning. 

Research has shown that incorporating Minecraft into educational delivery can 

lead to improved engagement and educational outcomes. 

The number of peer-reviewed articles that discuss Minecraft as an educational 

tool is significant. One systematic review¹ identified 42 papers that examined the 

use of Minecraft in educational settings, highlighting its benefits for mathematics, 

language development, science, history, and social skills such as communication 

and collaboration. 

A more recent 2025 systematic review by Slattery et al. examined the evidence 

base for Minecraft in education, identifying studies that reported positive effects 

on spatial thinking, creativity, critical thinking, mathematics, science, and 

language learning, though noting that rigorous replication studies are needed.² 

Importantly, students using Minecraft also demonstrate increased motivation, 

which is a significant predictor of academic achievement (Skinner & Pitzer, 2012). 

Furthermore, the interactive and enjoyable nature of Minecraft helps reduce

anxiety related to learning, particularly in subjects such as mathematics and 

science, making learning more accessible and enjoyable (Plass et al., 2015). 

Improving Motivation and Attendance 

One of the reasons why Minecraft increases motivation to learn is that it allows 

students to have autonomy, competence, and relatedness, which are the three 

psychological needs for intrinsic motivation according to self-determination 

theory (Ryan & Deci, 2000). 

Autonomy means that students can choose how to learn and what to create 

in Minecraft, which gives them a sense of ownership and agency. 

Competence means that students can master skills and challenges in 

Minecraft, which gives them a sense of accomplishment and confidence. 

Relatedness means that students can interact and collaborate with others in 

Minecraft, which gives them a sense of belonging and social support. 

These factors foster intrinsic motivation, which is more sustainable and effective 

than extrinsic motivation. 

Research has supported that Minecraft can enhance student motivation in various 

educational contexts. For example, Ke (2014) found that using Minecraft in an 

after-school program increased students' interest, enjoyment, and persistence in 

mathematics learning. Likewise, Short (2012) found that using Minecraft in a 

middle school English class increased students' engagement, participation, and 

collaboration in literacy activities. 

An important outcome of increased student motivation is increased attendance. 

When students are motivated to learn, they are more likely to attend school 

regularly and participate in class activities. Attendance is a key indicator of student 

success, as it affects academic achievement, social-emotional development, and 

future opportunities (Gottfried, 2010). 

Improving English Language Arts (ELA) Outcomes 

Minecraft supports English language arts (ELA) outcomes by fostering literacy 

skills, such as reading comprehension, writing, and vocabulary. Students can use 

Page 4 of 11  

Minecraft to create stories, narratives, and digital portfolios that demonstrate 

their understanding of texts and concepts. 

Research evidence supports the use of Minecraft in ELA outcomes by showing 

that it can improve students' reading and writing skills, as well as their 

engagement and motivation. Brownell (2021) examined how elementary students 

used Minecraft in their writing practices, finding that students blurred the 

boundaries between play and composition, using their Minecraft experiences to 

enhance creative writing and literacy development. 

Lewis Ellison, Evans, and Pike (2016) investigated how Minecraft supported 

language and literacy practices, finding that students benefited from using 

Minecraft to enhance learning in English Language Arts, with teachers successfully 

using the game to increase academic engagement.⁸ 

"Students can use Minecraft to create stories, narratives, and digital portfolios 

that demonstrate their understanding of texts and concepts, fostering creativity 

and critical thinking essential for ELA and beyond." - Lewis Ellison et al. (2016) 

Improving Mathematics Education Outcomes 

Minecraft supports mathematics outcomes by facilitating the development of 

numeracy skills, such as arithmetic, geometry, and measurement. Students can 

use Minecraft to explore mathematical concepts, solve problems, and apply their 

learning in authentic contexts. 

Foerster (2017) investigated using Minecraft to teach spatial geometry in grades 

5/6, studying two learning scenarios with 103 students. The research found that 

Minecraft allowed children to create three-dimensional objects in a constructive 

and algorithmic way, effectively bridging gaps in spatial geometry education. 

Andrus, Bar-El, Msall, Uttal, and Worsley (2020) examined spatial reasoning 

practices while 11 middle school students played Minecraft, identifying five in-

game practices that align with spatial reasoning development. The study found 

that Minecraft encouraged spatial manipulation of 3D objects and supported the 

development of transferable spatial skills. 

Carbonell-Carrera, Saorín, and Jaeger (2021) investigated the impact of a block-

building workshop using Minecraft on students' mental rotation skills. After the 

Page 5 of 11  

workshop, participants showed improvement on mental rotation tasks, with 

results comparable to other 3D modeling interventions. 

"The immersive and interactive nature of Minecraft can significantly enhance the 

educational experience in geometry and spatial reasoning, allowing students to 

visualize and manipulate mathematical concepts directly." - Foerster (2017) 

Nebel, Schneider, and Rey (2016) conducted a literature review on the use of 

Minecraft in education and research, finding evidence for its effectiveness in 

mathematics learning through experiential engagement with mathematical 

concepts. 

Improving Science Education Outcomes 

Minecraft can also improve science education outcomes in K-12 by providing a 

platform for students to engage in scientific inquiry, experimentation, and 

modeling. Several studies have shown that using Minecraft as a simulation tool 

can enhance students' understanding of scientific concepts. 

Hobbs et al. (2019) examined using Minecraft Education during a project called 

"Science Hunters" to teach environmental science concepts in schools, finding 

that students developed deeper understanding of ecological principles through 

hands-on virtual experimentation.¹³ 

Lane, Yi, Guerrero, and Comins (2017) explored Minecraft as a sandbox for STEM 

interest development, presenting preliminary results showing that Minecraft 

provided opportunities for students to engage with science concepts in creative 

and exploratory ways.¹⁴ 

"Minecraft provides a platform for students to design and test scientific scenarios, 

developing deeper understanding of principles and interactions while fostering 

appreciation for environmental and scientific issues." - Hobbs et al. (2019) 

Improving Digital Technology Skills 

Minecraft can improve digital literacy and digital skilling, which are essential 

competencies for the 21st century. By using Minecraft to create and explore 

digital worlds, students can develop their digital literacy skills, such as searching, 

accessing, analyzing, and synthesizing information from multiple sources. 

Page 6 of 11  

Peppler, Santo, Gresalfi, and Salen Tekinbaş (2019) examined scripting and coding 

in Minecraft, finding implications for computational literacy and creativity in both 

formal and informal learning environments. 

"Minecraft provides an opportunity for students to develop and practice a range of 

digital skills, such as using digital tools and applications, creating and sharing 

digital artifacts, and collaborating and communicating online. These skills are 

essential for students to participate and succeed in the digital society and 

economy." - Peppler et al. (2019) 

Baek, Min, and Yun (2020) examined the educational implications of Minecraft, 

finding that the game supported development of digital competencies and 21st-

century learning skills through creative problem-solving and digital construction. 

Improving Computer Technology and Computer Science Outcomes 

Minecraft can improve computer technology and computer science outcomes, 

such as programming skills, computational thinking, and digital literacy. By using 

Minecraft as a platform for creating and modifying games, simulations, and 

models, students can learn the basics of coding, logic, and problem-solving. 

Doran, Boyce, and Burke (2016) explored how using Minecraft to introduce 

Python programming to fifth-grade students influenced their interest and 

confidence in computer science. The study found that students who used 

Minecraft had higher levels of engagement, enjoyment, and self-efficacy in 

programming than students who used a traditional text-based environment. 

Grover and Pea (2013) provided a comprehensive review of computational 

thinking in K-12 education, establishing a framework for understanding how tools 

like Minecraft can support the development of computational thinking skills 

including abstraction, decomposition, and algorithm design. 

Supporting Students with Learning Disabilities 

Minecraft can support students with learning disabilities, such as dyslexia, 

dyscalculia, ADHD, and autism. By providing a multimodal and customizable 

platform, Minecraft can cater to the diverse needs and preferences of these 

students, enhancing their motivation, engagement, and self-efficacy. 

Page 7 of 11  

Zolyomi and Schmalz (2017) examined how autistic children used Minecraft to 

develop social skills in both home and therapy contexts, finding that the game 

provided a comfortable environment for practicing social interactions. 

Kilmer, Kilmer, and Bacevice (2023) described therapeutically applied Minecraft 

groups with neurodivergent youth, particularly those with autism and ADHD. The 

study found that Minecraft groups designed to support social engagement were 

particularly well-suited for neurodivergent youth, providing opportunities for peer 

connection and social practice in facilitated environments. 

Gerhardt et al. (2020) presented a case study exploring how Minecraft was 

adapted for trauma treatment for an 11-year-old child with Autism Spectrum 

Disorder. The narrative component allowed the child to process traumatic 

experiences in a structured, supportive format. 

"Minecraft can be an effective educational tool for children with learning 

disabilities, showcasing its adaptability and accessibility in providing supportive 

environments that reduce anxiety and facilitate creative expression." - Zolyomi & 

Schmalz (2017) 

Tabibi, Borzabadi, and Stavrinos (2021) investigated the impact of Minecraft on 

cognitive flexibility in students with ADHD, finding that the game improved 

cognitive flexibility measures compared to control groups. 

Fostering Advanced Skills Development 

Minecraft can support the development of higher-order skills, such as critical 

thinking, creativity, and collaboration. These skills are essential for students to 

succeed in the 21st century, as they enable them to analyze, synthesize, and 

evaluate information, generate novel ideas, and work effectively with others. 

Kafai, Burke, and Fields (2016) examined what happens when teachers use 

Minecraft game design as a context for learning, finding that the platform 

supported development of creativity, problem-solving, and collaborative skills 

through maker-oriented pedagogies.²³ 

Nebel et al. (2016) found in their literature review that Minecraft supported 

higher-order thinking skills through its open-ended nature, encouraging 

experimentation, exploration, and iterative design processes.¹²

"Minecraft facilitates not only individual learning but also collaborative skills, 

enhancing engagement and producing richer learning outcomes through shared 

projects and cooperative problem-solving." - Kafai et al. (2016) 

Developing Social Cohesion 

Minecraft can help develop social cohesion in young people by fostering a sense 

of community, identity, and belonging. The game enables students to create and 

inhabit virtual worlds that reflect their interests, values, and identities. 

Boyd et al. (2016) conducted a digital ethnography study in Autcraft, a Minecraft 

server for children with autism, examining how the community transformed the 

game into assistive technology. They found that players and administrators 

created supportive spaces that enhanced social engagement and community 

belonging.²⁴ 

Students who participated in Minecraft communities developed strong group 

identity and mutual support, as well as respect for diversity. Minecraft can be a 

powerful tool to enhance the social and emotional well-being of young people, 

especially those who may feel marginalized or isolated in their offline lives. 

Conclusion 

Minecraft offers diverse possibilities for enhancing educational outcomes across 

various disciplines. By leveraging its flexibility and engaging nature, educators can 

create a rich, interactive, and enjoyable learning environment. As educational 

practices evolve, Minecraft stands out as a valuable tool to supplement traditional 

educational methods, promoting an active and participatory learning experience.
