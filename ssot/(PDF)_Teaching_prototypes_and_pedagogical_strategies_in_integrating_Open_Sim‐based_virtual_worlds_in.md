Introduction Virtual worlds (VWs) are immersive three‐dimensional environments, accessible simultaneously to multiple users, and described as shared, simulated spaces, whose inhabitants represented as avatars. VWs enable freedom of expression. Using VWs in educational contexts requires a shift from teacher‐centered instruction to facilitation of student‐centered learning practices. Method This study explores the degree of teacher centrality (Sage‐on‐the‐stage, Facilitator, Guide‐on‐the‐side, or Partner), pedagogical strategies used in the integration of an Open Sim‐based VW learning platform in face‐to‐face K‐12 classrooms, and consistency between the perspectives of teachers and students on these topics. We conducted semi‐structured interviews followed by observations of actual VW environments with 12 elementary teachers of different subject‐matters and 11 fifth and sixth graders who learn with VWs. Findings Although all teaching prototypes were present in the data, the most dominant teaching prototypes in the VW environment were ‘Guide‐on‐the‐side’ and ‘Facilitator’, while ‘Sage‐on‐the‐stage’ and ‘Partner’ were less frequent. The findings indicate that the most prevalent pedagogical strategies used to teach in VW‐enhanced classrooms were Experiential Learning, followed by Training practice and modeling, Project‐based learning, Game‐based learning, and Collaborative learning. Minor differences were found between the perspectives of teachers and students regarding teaching prototypes and the degree of teacher centrality in the classroom. Importantly, both teachers and students positioned students as the main designers of learning activities in the VW environment. J   Comput   Assist   Learn.   2023;39:1141 – 1153.   wileyonlinelibrary.com/journal/jcal   11411 | INTRODUCTION The   adoption   and   integration   of   new   and   emerging   technologies, advanced   digital   tools,   and   innovative   environments   like   virtual   worlds (VWs)   have   the   potential   to   transform   educational   landscape and   change   learning   and   training   practices.   VWs   are   interactive three-dimensional   (3D)   or   animated   environments,   which   can   be simultaneously   explored   by   many   users.   These   worlds   can   be   used   in a   variety   of   educational   contexts   (Girvan   &   Savage,   2019 ;   Gong, 2018 ).   The   affordances   of   VWs   can   improve   face-to-face   or   digital learning   experiences   and   increase   motivation,   student   engagement, and   authentic   learning   across   different   disciplines   (Gregory   et   al., 2015 ).   The   vision   of   the   Metaverse,   a   new   integration   of   the   physical and   virtual   world   that   advances   the   development   of   new   immersive virtual   worlds   at   scale,   has   been   seen   as   a   promising   trend   of   the   edu- cation   in   the   future   (Dwivedi   et   al.,   2022 ;   Zhang   et   al.,   2022 ). The   Open   Sim   (Open   Simulator)   was   released   as   a   free,   open- source,   multi-user   3D   virtual   world   platform   in   2008   (Open   Simulator, 2019 ).   Open   Sim   technological   infrastructure   allows   construction   of private   or   public   three-dimensional   worlds   (islands),   accessible   via   the internet.   Numerous   studies   revealed   the   contribution   of   using   3D   VW platforms,   such   as   Open   Sim   and   Second   Life,   for   teaching   various   dis- ciplines   and   fields   of   knowledge   (for   review   see:   Reiso  glu   et   al.,   2017 ). From   a   pedagogical   point   of   view,   research   shows   that   virtual worlds   facilitate   pedagogical   innovations   (e.g.,   Girvan   &   Savage,   2019 ; Warburton,   2009 )   and   offer   opportunities   for   extending,   rethinking, and   envisioning   new   learning   environments   (Dawley   &   Dede,   2014 ; Dickey,   2011 ).   Seen   as   a   prominent   teaching   direction   (Gregory   et   al., 2019 ;   Grivokostopoulou   et   al.,   2020 ),   VWs   can   enrich   the   ways   we learn,   work,   and   play   (de   Freitas   &   Neumann,   2009 ).   Literature   on VWs   demonstrated   that   pedagogical   strategies,   such   as   exploration- based,   collaborative,   problem-based,   and   game-based   learning,   were used   intensively   in   3D   virtual   environments   (see:   Reiso  glu   et   al., 2017 ).   More   sophisticated   educational   use   of   VWs   includes   3D   con- tent   creation   (Christopoulos   et   al.,   2014 )   and   scripting   objects (Kluge   &   Riley,   2008 ). However,   integration   of   advanced   technologies,   such   as   VWs   per se,   does   not   necessarily   improve   teaching   and   learning   processes   or naturally   alter   the   instructional   strategies   into   more   student-centered learning   (Avidov-Ungar   et   al.,   2020 ;   Shamir-Inbal   &   Blau,   2017 ;   Hadad et   al.,   2020 ;   Ryberg,   2013 ).   Educators   who   integrate   VWs   into   their classroom   should   be   ready   to   become   facilitators   and   supporters   of activities   that   transform   students   from   passive   observers   and receivers   of   information   to   active   explorers   of   learning   environments (Zhao   et   al.,   2010 ).   Unfortunately,   research   indicated   (e.g.,   Guzzetti   & Stokrocki,   2013 )   that   even   when   using   innovation   technologies   such as   VWs,   some   teachers   still   employ   traditional   pedagogy.   In   order   to shift   to   more   student-centered   learning,   there   is   a   need   to   support teachers   in   re-designing   their   practices   (Blau   &   Shamir-Inbal,   2017 ; Hadad   et   al.,   2020 ;   Kasperski   &   Blau,   2020 ;   Shamir-Inbal   &   Blau, 2021 ).   Effective   integration   of   technology   in   the   classroom   demands awareness,   effort,   and   time   invested   in   effective   pedagogical   design that   transforms   students   from   novices   to   experts   in   cognitive   and social – emotional   digital   competencies   (Yondler   et   al.,   2018 ). According   to   González   et   al.   ( 2013 ),   utilizing   technology   in   teach- ing   can   be   related   to   a   shift   in   the   instructor's   role   from   transmission of   information   towards   learner-centered,   constructivist,   and   inquiry   or problem-based   pedagogies.   For   example,   the   research   showed   that instructors   who   adopted   student-centered   teaching   practices   were also   found   to   be   more   inclined   to   integrate   technology   into   their instruction   (Kale   &   Goh,   2014 ;   Ruggiero   &   Mong,   2015 ).   Studies   have also   demonstrated   that   teachers   adopt   different   teaching   types   in integration   of   technologies   in   learning   environments   (Kesler   et   al., 2022 ;   Prescott,   2014 ;   Quitadamo   &   Brown,   2001 ;   Weaver   & Nilson,   2005 ). This   study   explores   teaching   types   and   pedagogical   practices prevalent   in   integration   of   an   Open   Sim-based   virtual   world   in   K-12 settings.   We   specifically   aimed   to   understand   whether   this   innovative learning   environment   can   change   the   degree   of   teacher   centrality   in the   classroom   and   promote   more   student-centered   pedagogy   and active   designing   of   learning   activities   by   students. 1.1   | Literature   review Teaching   types   are   dimensions   of   attitudes,   beliefs,   needs,   and   behav- iours   of   educators   in   their   interactions   with   students   during   the teaching-learning   process   (Grasha,   2002 ),   which   are   reflected   in   the manner   of   designing   and   conducting   learning   activities   in   the   class- room   (Cooper,   2001 ).   Several   studies   explored   the   relationships between   teaching   types,   instructional   methods,   and   technology   inte- gration   (e.g.,   Chen,   2008 ;   Franzoni   &   Assar,   2009 ;   Ruggiero   &   Mong, 2015 ).   Regarding   characteristics   of   teaching   types,   Englund   ( 2017 ) found   that   in   integration   of   VWs   in   higher   education,   the   majority   of teachers   adopted   the   role   of   facilitator   and   mediator.   Mørch   et   al. ( 2018 )   reported   that   in   an   online   teacher   preparation   program   in   a VW   learning   environment,   instructors   provided   social – emotional, technological,   and   pedagogical   scaffolding   to   promote   collaborative learning.   Both   studies   emphasized   that   the   teaching   staff   integrated VWs   with   student-centered   pedagogy   appropriate   for   this   learning context.   However,   the   research   concerning   teacher   types   in   integra- tion   of   VWs   is   scarce,   especially   in   the   K-12   context A   conceptual   model   of   five   major   teaching   styles   was   introduced by   Grasha   ( 1994 , 2002 )   without   reference   to   technology   integration. Taking   into   consideration   the   interaction   of   teachers   and   students, the   model   categorizes   instructional   roles   on   a   spectrum   of   teacher- centered   and   student-centered   behaviours.   Each   teaching   style   is associated   with   particular   pedagogical   designs   and   instructional   strat- egies. In   discussing   teaching-learning-assessment   in   technology- enhanced   K-12   environments,   a   more   recent   studies   (Hadad   et   al., 2020 ;   Kesler   et   al.,   2022 ;   Yondler   et   al.,   2018 ;   Yondler   &   Blau,   2021 ) revealed   four   prototypes   of   teachers   that   reflect   their   degree   of   cen- trality   in   the   classroom:   (1)   the   teacher   as   a   sage-on-the-stage,   (2)   a facilitator,   (3)   a   guide-on-the-sid e,   and   (4)   a   partner.   Sage-on-the- stage   refers   to   a   teacher   prototype,   which   is   dominant   in   the   learn- ing   processes   and   characterized   by   a   belief   that   teachers   must   pro- vide   students   with   clear   direct   instructions   and   determine   learning activities,   with   little   room   for   learn ers'   independence.   The   facilita- tor   prototype   is   characterized   by   a   certain   degree   of   centrality   in the   teaching-learning   process,   yet   offers   r estricted   opportunities for   experimentation   and   active   participation   of   student s   combined with   significant   teacher   scaffolding.   The   guide-on-the-si de   proto- type   organizes   and   coordinates   the   learning   process,   helps   and guides   students,   and   offers   them   opportunities   for   unstructured exploration   and   experimentation.   This   prototype   lets   learners choose   their   own   learning   path   and   encourages   them   to   actively construct   their   knowledge.   Finally,   the   Partne r   prototype   goes beyond   open   exploration   and   self-construction   of   knowledge.   S/he establishes   a   culture   of   community   and   collaboration   in   the   class- room,   promoting   teacher-student   equality,   a   partnership   relation- ship   with   the   students,   and   s/he   is   open   to   learn   together   with   and even   from   his/her   students. Please   note   that   teacher   prototypes   can   be   expressed   in   a   variety of   subject   matter.   For   example,   if   the   teacher   is   a   facilitator,   students can   improve   their   spelling   in   language   arts   or   in   a   second   language class   by   a   book   reading   assignment   followed   by   writing   dictations   with vocabulary   common   in   that   book.   However,   if   the   teacher   is   a   guide- on-the-side,   as   a   part   of   project-based   learning,   students   can   be assigned   to   type   an   essay   on   a   topic   of   their   choice.   After   researching the   chosen   topic,   students   will   write   the   essay   and   improve   their   spell- ing   gradually   and   naturally   by   using   the   spelling   check   feature   of   a word   processing   software   and   correcting   their   texts.   Similarly,   math can   be   taught   using   different   degrees   of   teacher   centrality   in   the classroom.   For   example,   if   the   teacher   is   a   sage-on-the-stage,   s/he   will teach   through   direct   instruction   following   by   drill   and   practice.   In   con- trast,   if   the   teacher   functions   as   a   partner,   students   can   learn   a   topic through   the   flipping   classroom   approach   and   design   digital   games   for peer   practice   using   an   application   of   their   choice.   Thus,   teachers   of the   same   subject-matter   working   with   students   of   the   same   academic level   can   reflect   different   degrees   of   their   centrality   in   the   classroom. Different   teacher   prototypes   will   use   different   pedagogical   strate- gies   (Yondler   &   Blau,   2021 ;   Yondler   et   al.,   2018 ),   and   this   might   also be   reflected   in   advanced   learning   environments   such   as   VW. However,   less   is   known   regarding   how   teachers use   these   advanced   technologies   in   their   classroom   and   what   teaching types   are   being   adopted   in   the   blended   learning   environment.   This research   examined   the   teaching   types   employed   by   teachers   when   an Open   Sim-based   virtual   world   was   used   in   the   classroom,   the   peda- gogical   strategies   and   learning   characteristics   that   are   prevalent   in integration   of   VWs   in   formal   education,   and   the   consistency   between the   perspectives   of   teachers   and   students. The   first   research   question   explored   which   teaching   prototypes are   adopted   by   educators   who   integrate   VWs   in   K-12   settings (reflecting   the   degree   of   teacher   centrality   in   the   classroom).   The   find- ings   revealed   all   four   prototypes   found   by   Yondler   et   al.   ( 2018 )   in   the general   context   of   technology-enhanced   classrooms   and   recently   by other   authors   in   the   context   of   visual   programming   (Hadad   et   al., 2020 ;   Kesler   et   al.,   2022 ):   Sage-on-the-stage,   Facilitator,   Guide-on- the-side,   and   Partner.   However,   only   partial   correspondence   was found   with   the   Grasha   ( 2002 )   framework.   Namely,   among   three   roles that   corresponded   to   the   Sage-on-the-stage   style,   two   were   absent   in our   data:   Expert   and   Formal   authority.   The   teachers   in   this   study could   demonstrate   personal   modelling   of   learning   with   VW,   but   the vast   majority   were   neither   authority   on   the   topic   of   VWs,   nor   pos- sessed   sufficient   expertise   in   designing   learning   activities   in   this environment. Importantly,   our   findings   revealed   that   teachers   apply   a   variety   of teaching   prototypes   in   using   VWs.   These   findings   align   with   Grasha's conclusion   (Grasha,   1994 , 2002 ;   Yondler   et   al.,   2018 )   that   teaching prototypes   are   not   mutually   exclusive   categories.   Rather,   teachers adopt   different   roles   for   different   pedagogical   purposes,   while   some of   them   become   more   dominant   and   others   more   peripheral   (Grasha, 2002 ).   Adaptability   to   different   teaching   types   is   an   important   tool that   prepares   educators   for   a   variety   of   teaching   conditions   and   for addressing   the   needs   of   a   greater   variety   of   learners   (Cendon,   2018 ; Grasha,   2002 ;   Vaughn   &   Baker,   2001 ). According   to   our   findings,   the   most   prevalent   teaching   prototype was   Guide-on-the-side   prototype,   followed   by   Facilitator , Partner ,   and finally   Sage   on   the   Stage .   These   findings   suggest   that   most   teachers manage   their   VW-enhanced   classroom   by   using   the   constructivist pedagogical   approach   and   student-centered   experiences.   In   construc- tivist   pedagogy   a   teacher   orchestrates   the   environment,   guides   stu- dents   to   develop   and   refine   their   thinking,   encourages   and   values student   questions   and   input   to   promote   reflective   thinking,   and   pro- vides   opportunities   for   students   to   create   meaning   through   active   and relevant   experiences   (Baxter-Magolda,   2000 ;   Karakus   et   al.,   2016 ). Previous   findings   (Englund,   2017 )   complement   our   results,   indicating   a change   in   teacher   strategies   from   conveying   information   towards   a more   student-centered   approach,   and   the   use   of   problem-based   activ- ities   when   teaching   in   virtual   worlds.   Similarly,   an   examination   of   ped- agogical   approaches   of   teachers   who   teach   creative   computing   using the   Scratch   online   platform   showed   the   integration   of   constructivist teaching   strategies   by   both   teachers   with   constructivist   and   teachers with   instructivist   pedagogical   perspectives   (Kesler   et   al.,   2019 , 2022 ; Yondler   et   al.,   2018 ). It   is   important   to   note   that   the   type   of   distribution   for   teaching methods   presented   a   bell-shaped   curve.   Namely,   the   vast   majority   of teachers   are   in   the   middle,   functioning   as   Facilitator   or   Guide-on-the- side ,   while   Sage-on-the-stage   and   Partner   were   less   common.   Impor- tantly,   over   time,   some   teachers   changed   their   teaching   practices   with the   virtual   environment   and   slightly   shifted   their   role   to   more student-centered.   However,   this   progress   is   mainly   from   Sage-on-the- stage   to   Facilitator ,   and,   in   some   cases,   from   Guide-on-the-side   to   Part- ner .   This   means   that   despite   certain   decentralization,   teachers remained   within   the   same   paradigm.   We   could   not   find   evidence   for   a shift   from   an   ‘ instructivist ’ approach   to   a   “ constructivist ” approach. The   second   research   question   e xplored   which   pedagogical strategies   and   learning   characteristics   are   prevalent   in   integrati on of   VWs   in   formal   education.   Our   findings   show   that   most   teachers described   Experiential   Learning   as   the   main   instructional   method adopted   in   their   teaching   with   the   Eureka   World   learning   platform. This   was   followed   by   Training   pra ctice   and   modelling,   Project- based   learning,   Game-based   learning,   and   finally,   Collaboration. Over   a   quarter   of   the   strategies   (26%)   use   more   traditional   teaching methods,   such   as   demonstration   and   practice,   while   other   strate- gies   in   the   data   reflect   a   student-centered   approach.   Our   findings correspond   with   previous   results,   indicating   t he   effectiveness   of VWs   for   facilitating   project-based   experiential   learning   (Jarmon et   al.,   2009 ).   Experiential   learning   highlights   construction   of   knowl- edge   through   experience   rather   than   on   learning   information   or remembering   facts.   Moreover,   such   learning   can   include   mor e   per- sonalized   activities   and   tasks   relat ed   to   learners'   interests,   and   thus, improve   transfer   of   knowledge   and   skills   from   one   context   to another   (de   Freitas   &   Neumann,   2009 ). PORAT   ET   AL . 1149Interestingly,   despite   the   fact   that   participating   teachers   come from   diverse   subject-matters   and   schools,   all   based   their   teaching   on the   same   instructional   methods.   Since   all   the   teachers   in   the   study participated   in   the   same   preliminary   training   course,   we   assume   that the   course   might   serve   as   a   model   for   teachers.   The   course   was   based mostly   on   two   instructional   methods   -   experiential   hands-on   training lessons   and   assignments,   and   a   major   project-based   task   of   designing and   developing   a   learning   unit   in   the   VW   environment.   It   could   be assumed   then,   that   teachers   might   have   adopted   and   transferred   to the   classroom   the   same   instructional   methods   their   lecturer   practiced in   the   training   course.   This   assumption   is   also   supported   by   previous research   (Nussli   &   Oh,   2015 )   that   aimed   to   determine   the   effective- ness   of   the   teacher   training   workshop   about   using   VWs   for   education. During   the   workshop   the   instructors   incorporated   modelling   by   direct examples:   how   to   divide   responsibilities,   use   VWs   in   the   classroom, and   address   technical   issues.   It   seems   that   participants   modelled   the instructor's   behaviour   and   felt   confident   about   their   ability   to   handle issues   that   may   arise   in   a   similar   manner. The   third   research   question   examined   whether   there   is   consis- tency   between   the   perspectives   of   teachers   and   students   regarding the   role   of   the   teacher   in   VWs,   pedagogical   and   the   instructional methods   used   in   the   classroom.   Our   data   indicates   similarities between   teacher   and   student   perspectives   on   the   role   of   the   teacher, with   students   describing   a   slightly   more   teacher-centered   approach and   a   higher   degree   of   teacher   centrality   in   the   classroom.   Prior   find- ings   also   point   to   a   perception   gap   between   teachers   and   students regarding   online   (Osborne   et   al.,   2009 )   or   blended   teaching   and   learn- ing   (Miyazoe   et   al.,   2010 ). Importantly,   students'   epistemological   beliefs   on   the   nature   of knowledge   and   learning   processes   might   also   be   reflected   in   their   per- spectives   on   the   degree   of   teacher   centrality   in   the   classroom   and   the way   that   knowledge   should   be   achieved.   The   belief   in   the   teacher   as an   expert   and   authority   and   the   assumption   that   knowledge   is   objec- tive,   was   found   to   relate   positively   to   a   traditional   perspective   on teaching   and   learning   (Otting   et   al.,   2010 ).   On   the   contrary,   students who   attribute   study   success   to   their   own   learning   efforts   and   do   not believe   in   the   innateness   and   certainty   of   knowledge,   are   likely   to   hold a   constructivist   perception   of   teaching   and   learning. As   mentioned   above,   an   interesting   trend   which   emerged   from the   interviews   was   that   some   teachers   spontaneously   reported   a gradual   shift   to   a   slightly   more   student-centered   position.   This   finding is   consistent   with   previous   ones   showing   that   a   learning   environment where   a   student   is   active   and   creates   and   learns   independently,   there is   a   gradual   transition   from   a   traditional   instructivist   pedagogical approach   to   a   constructivist   pedagogical   approach   (Kesler   et   al., 2022 ).   This   means   that   the   student   learning   culture   also   required   a change   so   that   students   could   play   an   active   role   in   the   learning   pro- cess,   rather   than   being   recipients   of   information   from   the   teacher (Keiler,   2018 ).   It   is   therefore   crucial   that   instructors   adjust   their   roles and   adopt   a   less   centralized   approach   when   using   these   student- designed   learning   activities   in   the   classroom. Notably,   our   findings   indicate   that   all   pedagogical   strategies reported   by   teachers   were   supported   by   examples   of   classroom activities   provided   by   the   students.   Moreover,   each   of   these   strate- gies   was   consistent   with   the   VW   literature   examining   project-based learning   (See:   Chen   &   Yang,   2019 ;   Jarmon   et   al.,   2008 ),   experiential learning   (de   Freitas   &   Neumann,   2009 ;   Jarmon   et   al.,   2009 ),   game- based   learning   (Terzidou   et   al.,   2012 ;   Young   et   al.,   2012 ),   and   collabo- rative   learning   (Cho   &   Lim,   2017 ).   However,   the   current   study explored   pedagogical   strategies   comprehensively   and   based   them   on triangulation   between   student   and   teacher   data. Examining   the   characteristics   of   the   VW   learning   environment, we   found   that   both   teachers   and   learners   described   situated   cognition and   connecting   to   real   life/authentic   learning   as   important   pedagogi- cal   attributes   of   the   environment.   It   seems   that   VWs   can   promote authentic   learning   and   situated   cognition,   in   which   learners   take   an active   role   to   solve   meaningful   real-world   tasks,   problems,   and   scenar- ios.   Once   they   engage   themselves   with   the   simulated   environment   in a   way   that   resembles   real-life   situations   and   communication,   learners construct   knowledge   through   interaction   with   objects   and   events   in the   VW   as   in   the   real   world   and   develop   new   skills   leading   to   situated cognition   (Ata,   2016 ;   Farley,   2016 ). In   addition,   both   teachers   and   students   stressed   such   characteris- tics   of   learning   in   VWs   as   flow,   immersion,   and   enhanced   learning motivation   during   engaging   in   VW   experiences.   These   results   match previous   studies.   VWs   enhance   learning   experiences   and   learner   moti- vation   (Huang   et   al.,   2019 ;   Tilhou   et   al.,   2020 ).   Flow   experience, including   intense   involvement,   focused   attention   and   losing   a   sense   of time,   has   been   found   to   enhance   positive   attitudes   and   satisfaction from   the   learning   experience   in   VWs   (Hassell   et   al.,   2012 )   and   plays   a supporting   role   in   strengthening   self-efficacy   of   academic   perfor- mance   (Li   et   al.,   2012 ).   Immersion   experiences   relate   to   the   feeling   of being   absorbed   in   the   virtual   environment   and   a   strong   sense   that   you are   ‘ really   there ’ ,   and   just   as   in   real   life,   exploring   contexts   and   per- forming   activities   and   social   interactions.   Recent   studies   have   shown that   immersion   has   improved   learning   outcomes   and   learning   motiva- tion,   as   compared   to   learning   with   representation   of   the   same   envi- ronment   without   such   an   immersive   experience   (Lan   et   al.,   2019 ; Lan   &   Liao,   2018 ). 5 | CONCLUSION   AND   IMPLICATIONS The   current   study   contributes   to   the   still   scarce   literature   on   pedagog- ical   strategies   and   instructional   roles   in   VW   learning   environments. This   pioneer   research   examined   the   teaching   prototypes   and   the degree   of   teacher   centrality   in   the   VW   learning   environment. Research   was   based   on   the   suggested   comprehensive   model   of teacher   prototypes   in   a   technology-enhanced   environment,   a   model which   integrated   Grasha's   conceptual   model   of   teaching   types (Grasha,   1994 , 2002 )   as   well   as   Kesler   et   al.   ( 2022 ),   Yondler   and   Blau ( 2021 )   and   Yondler   et   al.   ( 2018 )   prototypes   of   teachers.   Future   study may   replicate   the   model   of   teacher   prototypes   in   other   types   of technology-enhanced   environments   to   understand   and   reflect   upon their   role   and   the   degree   of   teacher   centrality   in   the   classroom   in   the eyes   of   educators   and   their   students. 1150   PORAT   ET   AL .Our   findings   also   provide   insights   that   can   help   improve   profes- sional   training   courses   in   virtual   3D   environments   and   similar   emerg- ing   educational   technologies.   We   suggest   that,   alongside   technical aspects   of   using   VWs   for   teaching,   teacher-training   courses   should emphasize   constructivist   and   constructionist   pedagogical   methods that   are   grounded   in   research   and   can   promote   student-centered learning. 5.1   | Limitations   and   future   directions This   study   was   based   on   teacher   and   student   perceptions   collected through   semi-structured   interviews   and   guided,   follow-up   observa- tions   in   the   VW   environment.   However,   we   did   not   observe   teachers' roles   and   actual   pedagogical   methods   they   used;   thus,   we   cannot crosscheck   teacher   perspectives   with   their   actual   behaviour   in   the classroom.   Future   studies   may   triangulate   teacher   self-reports,   not only   with   observations   of   VW   learning   activities   designed   by   teachers, students,   and   external   experts,   but   also   with   non-participant   observa- tions   of   actual   teaching-learning   processes   in   the   classroom. With   regard   to   the   teacher   training   course,   as   mentioned   above, the   training   was   conducted   by   the   same   instructor   and   relied   mainly on   two   methods:   hands-on   training   lessons   and   project-based learning,   and   building   a   virtual   learning   unit.   It   is   important   that   pro- fessional   development   for   technology-enhanced   or   online   learning uses   a   variety   of   pedagogical   methods;   otherwise   the   training   might have   a   limiting   effect   on   teachers'   pedagogical   repertoire.
