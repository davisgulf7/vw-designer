Abstract 

In this literature review of recent research in the field of virtual worlds and in particular Minecraft Education, the video game and education software produced by Microsoft, Incorporated, the dynamic relationship between technology and pedagogy is examined, emphasizing the potential and challenges of this incredibly popular integrating emerging digital software in educational settings. As we find ourselves at the precipice of an educational transformation fueled by technology, the challenge lies not just in harnessing tools such as Minecraft for enhanced learning outcomes and experiences but also in understanding their practical and beneficial applications in diverse educational environments. A review of academic literature reveals the unanimous agreement on the promise of Minecraft in education. However, there is a shared sentiment emphasizing the need for empirical research, structured implementation frameworks, and pedagogical involvement and redesign to ensure these technologies’ effective incorporation. It is crucial to understand how Minecraft can align with various disciplines, educational contexts, and levels to maximize their potential. In this literature review I venture into this new educational landscape, a combined interdisciplinary approach, merging technological innovation with pedagogical expertise, is essential for crafting tools that are both advanced in capability and educationally effective.

Introduction 

With the spread of information technology in the classroom in the 21st century, little by little, through design software, CD-ROM, the internet, and various web technologies, the tools available allowed educators to teach the content of their syllabuses in different ways, and in the process also holding the possibility of expanding the content for students beyond the limits of what had been possible to do in a traditional classroom setting. 

I have been experimenting with various types of technologies in my classes to expand the horizons of the topics I teach, especially in cultural studies settings. One of the most prominent examples to arrive in recent years is Minecraft, a sandbox video game first released by Mojang Studios (now part of Microsoft, Inc.) in 2011 (although it was available in beta form since 2009). The game, or as I want to posit in this paper, the piece of software, allows players to explore procedurally generated 3D worlds made up of blocks representing different materials, including dirt, stone, water, and ores. The game’s mechanics encourage exploration, creativity, and survival. Players can mine resources, build structures, craft items, and interact with the environment in various ways. With its open-ended design, Minecraft offers a platform for multiple gameplay styles, including survival mode (where players must gather resources and fend off enemies) and creative mode (where players have unlimited resources to freely build).

While ostensibly a piece of entertainment software that has seen significant success critically and financially, for educational and academic purposes, Minecraft has frequently been cited as a tool for education, particularly in promoting spatial reasoning, problem-solving, and collaborative learning. Its adaptability allows educators to create immersive, interactive environments in which students can simulate historical events, explore mathematical concepts, or learn about environmental sustainability. The game’s potential for virtual world creation has led to the development of custom educational maps and scenarios, making it a valuable resource for both formal and informal learning settings. 

Because of its flexibility and widespread use, Minecraft has become a significant case study in educational research, particularly in areas such as gamification, student engagement, and virtual worlds in pedagogy. Upon observing the popularity of Minecraft in educational settings, given its status as a piece of technology that is as pedagogically potent as it is technologically sophisticated, Microsoft designed a version of the game specifically for use in educational settings in 2016, available on Microsoft Windows, Android, MacOS, iPadOS, and ChromeOS, with free lesson plans available online, two free companion applications: Code Connection and Classroom Mode, and an optional three-step training program for educators to familiarize themselves with the technology, recommended educational methods, and expand the use of Minecraft through their own technology.

Methodology 

There have been previous literature reviews of the research in this area (Nebel et al., 2015), and my aim with this paper is to build upon and to clarify issues that were previously uncertain in the literature. In particular, Nebel and colleagues were preoccupied with the concept that Minecraft could be further enhanced as a tool to incorporate relatively more complex programming knowledge into educational materials, but my research has found that, while programming has certainly benefited from applications using Minecraft, the role of the software as a virtual world for students to use has also expanded into interactive demonstration lessons, interactive historical experiences, and architectural playgrounds. This study scrutinizes the existing literature regarding the use of Minecraft in various fields with an emphasis on education to provide an overview of current research done in the field in the region of Oceania. This study utilized a systematic literature review to explore the applications of Minecraft in education, with a particular focus on its use in New Zealand and Oceania (in particular Polynesia).

Their research investigates the use of Minecraft as a virtual world that encourages creative play and imagination in children. The article discusses how Minecraft’s open-ended, sandbox environment allows for exploration, problem-solving, and creativity, making it a valuable tool for educational purposes. Monteiro’s results reaffirm the effectiveness of Minecraft as a tool in education, and, similarly to Cipollone et al.’s work, re-introduced the idea that adding storytelling elements can further motivate students and enhance the learning process. This highlights the potential of combining narrative elements with game-based learning to create more meaningful educational experiences.

While it can be used for activities and courses in the national curriculum, it can also be used for experiential learning in the day-to-day life of students, as can be seen in multiple studies focusing on disaster preparation (particularly in light of the Christchurch earthquake in 2011). Building on top of Findlay’s (2017) work about how digital games, particularly through concepts like gamification and augmented reality, can be powerful tools for education and can similarly promote preparedness by making learning about disasters more interactive and engaging, Le Dé et al. (2021) examined the use of LEGO and Minecraft to foster children’s participation in disaster risk reduction (DRR). In their study, 90 children aged 8-13 in Maraekakaho, New Zealand, utilized participatory mapping activities where students mapped their local surroundings and assessed potential hazards using LEGO and Minecraft. The study applied the “4 Ps” framework—Participants, Play, Process, and Power— to highlight the importance of children’s active involvement in designing and implementing DRR strategies. The findings show that Minecraft, alongside LEGO, is an effective tool for engaging children in DRR, providing a playful, hands-on approach to learning about hazards and disaster preparedness. The study emphasizes that integrating play in participatory DRR activities can empower children, helping them to influence decisions that impact their communities. This child-centered approach fosters a sense of ownership, encouraging proactive participation in disaster resilience initiatives. Gamepell et al. (2024) also found similar results in their study. Their work focused on the use of Minecraft Education to teach Norwegian lower secondary school students about indigenous peoples, focusing on the Māori of New Zealand. Conducted over five weeks, the study involved two eighth-grade classes using the pre-made “Ngā Motu” world within ME. This world allowed students to explore Māori culture, traditions, and history through interactive gameplay. The study found that Minecraft Education was an effective tool for teaching about indigenous cultures, particularly in terms of increasing student engagement and motivation. It also offered additional benefits, such as supporting social and emotional learning and enhancing language acquisition. Students who typically struggled with traditional teaching methods showed greater interest and enthusiasm when using the software. However, as has been a running theme of this literature review, the teacher’s role in guiding and facilitating the learning process was crucial to the success of the intervention. Much like other papers in this review, the research concludes that Minecraft can serve as a valuable educational tool when integrated into a curriculum, but it requires careful planning and teacher involvement to ensure students achieve meaningful learning outcomes. 

Conclusion 

In conclusion, the integration of Minecraft and Minecraft Education into classrooms worldwide, including in New Zealand, marks a significant transformation in educational methodologies. The reviewed literature

highlights Minecraft’s open-ended, creative, and collaborative environment as a powerful tool for fostering student engagement, enhancing problem-solving skills, and developing twenty-first-century competencies. Its versatility enables use across various subjects, including math, science, history, and language arts, promoting interdisciplinary learning. Studies in this review demonstrate that Minecraft Education creates immersive learning environments that motivate students, supporting both independent exploration and collaborative learning. In New Zealand, Minecraft has proven especially valuable for fostering cultural awareness and inclusivity, particularly in teaching indigenous knowledge and disaster preparedness. 

Despite its many advantages, challenges remain in the widespread adoption of Minecraft in education. These include technological barriers, the need for comprehensive teacher training, and aligning its use with curriculum goals and learning outcomes. Furthermore, many educators currently adapt Minecraft to traditional, teacher-centered methodologies, where knowledge transfer is largely one-directional. While this approach has yielded notable successes—such as teaching the Māori language, promoting cultural heritage awareness, and encouraging social citizenship—the potential of interactive virtual mediums like Minecraft extends far beyond these applications. The literature suggests that embracing more student-driven approaches could unlock deeper, more holistic learning experiences. Donkin’s (2017) experiments in virtual worlds and Gee’s (2003) theories on interactive education propose a paradigm where virtual platforms foster dynamic, reciprocal learning relationships between students and educators. Malmstrom’s (2018) principles—such as granting students agency, involving them in learning design, and focusing on creating content—further underline the transformative potential of Minecraft when used strategically.
