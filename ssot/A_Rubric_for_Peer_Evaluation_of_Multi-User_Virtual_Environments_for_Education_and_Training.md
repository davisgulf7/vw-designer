## Abstract

In a media-saturated online ecosystem, educational technology that fosters virtual interactions and learning opportunities, unlike those taking place face-to-face, has to bear special characteristics that facilitate the way we build our connection with others or access, consume, and produce new information. The present study focuses on the design and implementation of a rubric for the peer assessment of collaborative educational Virtual Reality (VR) environments that were built with the aim to provide immersive-triggered meaningful learning instances. It presents the methodology employed to create the tool, its use in peer evaluation processes, and the implementation findings. The stages of the methodology employed involve the review of existing tools, the rationale lying in the creation of the certain tool, and the recruitment of educators and/or trainers to pilot test it. To this end, there was a purposeful recruitment of participants of a postgraduate program in immersive technologies, with diverse demographics and from different disciplines, who were invited to work collaboratively, in pairs or groups of three, with the intent to design and develop an educational intervention of their choice in Spatial.io software. The stages of the methodology further involved microteaching sessions with other groups, peer evaluation based on the quality criteria provided, and self-reflection and evaluation of their educational interventions. The study outcomes revealed (i) the key evaluation criteria that proved to be critical for the design of quality immersive experiences, (ii) the usefulness of the rubric created to facilitate the pilot testing of the prototypes, and (iii) challenges and benefits that arise from peer evaluation practices. In the context of interdisciplinary, diverse age and professional experience demographics peer evaluation, digital, content, and pedagogical concerns arose providing fruitful feedback to their peers for the refinement of the design of their VR environments. Challenges and recommendations of the peer review processes are also discussed.

## 1. Introduction

In a rapidly evolving information society, trends in the deployment of Virtual Reality (VR) multi-user platforms as learning and training environments call for the design of spaces that foster meaningful learning while interacting with others in a virtual space. The design of information and contextualization of abstract notions in virtual learning environments should be tailored to learners' needs with the intent to enhance learning opportunities in an innovative but simultaneously inclusive educational setting. The learning content in virtual reality spaces may address different sensory channels, thus multiple information processing systems. Albus et al.'s [ 1] study stresses the need for instructional aid in VR spaces to help the learner select, organize, and integrate the relevant information. This involves selecting and providing only the necessary information, which is a key advantage of informatics over traditional face-to-face interactions. In light of this, when using instructional technology, there is a need for careful design and teaching competencies that purposefully provide the necessary information in alignment with the learning goals and objectives [ 2]. From the lens of deep learning opportunities, Lazou and Tsinakos's [ 3] study has identified pedagogical and instructional design concerns to foster meaningful learning instances in immersive environments that trigger learners' attention and facilitate digital well-being, which they have coined under the term Critical Immersive-Triggered Literacy (CITL). CITL, as a literacy framework, builds on learners' need to cultivate skills of attentional literacy in a multimodal space where the learning objects consist of three-dimensional digital media as a novel modality for information and knowledge building.

A systematic review of empirical studies that focused on the use of immersive VR in collaborative learning environments highlights the limited research available on how these technologies can be effectively designed and applied in educational and professional settings [ 4]. It conceptualizes collaborative learning in VR as a shared experience within an immersive space, emphasizing the importance of designing technologies and activities that enhance participation and social interaction among learners, while also addressing current challenges related to usability, realism, and social interaction facilitation. In the immersive design and activity-building process, peers working on the same task can offer and receive valuable feedback as an evaluation process that involves human-centered experience in VR, prioritizing user needs and preferences. Peer evaluation in higher education and training offers considerable transferable learning opportunities, like teamwork and critical thinking, while generating and evaluating discipline-specific information. It differs from instructors' evaluation of work and enhances students' evaluative judgment, an emerging notion that denotes an individual's ability to assess the quality of their own and others' work, essential for academic and future employment success [ 5, 6]. Peer assessments allow students to engage actively in the learning process and provide feedback [ 7] while enhancing their achievement and motivation through self-monitoring [ 8], responding to the discrepancies between self and the expected outcome [ 9].

However, van den Berg et al. [ 10] observe that the efficacy of peer evaluation also depends on the combination of the design characteristics employed. As a result, several frameworks and guidelines have been formulated for implementing peer review evaluations in higher education [ 7, 10, 11, 12]. Peer assessments need clear criteria to ensure that student feedback is accurate and valuable [ 13]. Adachi et al.'s [ 11] study yielded fruitful results with the identification of nineteen (19) peer assessment design elements clustered into six categories, namely, (i) decisions concerning the use of peer assessment; (ii) link between peer assessment and other elements in the learning environment; (iii) interaction between peers; (iv) composition of assessment groups; (v) management of the assessment procedure; and (vi) contextual elements. Hadzhikoleva et al.'s [ 14] study stresses the importance of the assessment procedure, namely, the time and manner of conducting the assessment; whether the assessment may be synchronous or asynchronous; anonymous or non-anonymous; performed in a dialogue with the author of the object being assessed, etc. The researchers add that the evaluators should be acquainted with the basic principles of assessment, namely, objectivity, ethics, transparency, adequacy, and responsibility, while [ 15] additionally note that on completion of the peer assessment process, peer feedback should be screened, filtered, and moderated by all involved in the process.

In higher education and training, Lazou et al.'s [ 16] study findings suggest that the collaborative process of designing and building activities within a context of continuous peer tutoring and assessment created a community of practice among pre-service undergraduate and in-service teachers that, as participants stated, “was an innovative approach of collaboration that they had not experienced in the past” (p. 58). In this study, the ultimate goal was the creation of materials that could be checked for their high-order critical thinking and creativity in the Community of Inquiry CoI framework, which, as [ 17] contend, is a promising framework based on a collaborative constructivist principle that facilitates critical discourse. It was also noticed that while exchanging ideas on creating their lesson plans, they reflected on their own work and adopted some of their peers' good practices to improve their work. In light of this, the element of reusability was employed in the framework of the produsage concept coined by [ 18] and collective intelligence [ 19] content creation that emerges from the collaboration, collective efforts, and consensus decision-making in contemporary digital media-informed societies. Additionally, ref. [ 20] contends that peer assessment as an alternative form of assessment provides opportunities for deep learning, constituting a form of “assessment for learning”, transforming the learner process from teacher-centered to student-centered. In the context of technology-enhanced adult education and training, systematic reviews conducted by [ 21, 22, 23] note that there is a need to further investigate in longitudinal studies how trainers and knowledge providers can handle and accommodate cognitive, affective, and social issues as well as collaborative learning in the new social VR platforms.

Based on what previous studies suggest and our need to examine the application of the framework introduced by our 2023 study, that is, the Critical Immersive-Triggered (CIT) Literacy [ 3] in collaborative VR environments for education and training, we need to review existing assessment tools so as to adopt, modify, or enrich them. CIT Literacy is defined as a skill development framework that triggers learners' attention and facilitates digital well-being for meaningful learning instances via immersive technologies. On the premise that the implementation of metaverse-based educational scenarios is still in its infancy but has the potential to improve the effectiveness of online learning experiences, it is essential that our study explore the complex framework lying behind designing and developing educational experiences in VR spaces, based on the learners' and users experiences (UX) and feedback on the quality of the space as an educational setting for authentic, deep learning instances, thus in alignment with the CITL framework. Our study will focus on the following research questions:

RQ1: What are the key design features of a peer evaluation rubric that effectively support the assessment of educational VR environments?

RQ2: How can a rubric with specific evaluation criteria facilitate the improved design and development of VR educational spaces?

RQ3: What challenges and benefits arise from the implementation of the rubric for peer evaluation in educational VR environments?

In an era that learning settings try to respond to the transformation of education not as a system of rote transfer of knowledge but as a systematic effort to provide holistic skillset development across disciplines and metacognition, we must find ways to define the key design features for effective collaborative VR learning spaces that will promote meaningful immersive-triggered learning. The user is at the center of interest, and as such, peer feedback for the refinement of a space will be based on their experiences, needs, and preferences while exploring it through the lens of the user and their post-experience reflection through the lens of the purposeful reviewer. To this end, the paper will review the existing rubrics and quality criteria for the evaluation of VR environments for educational and training purposes so as to propose a rubric that meets the needs for user-centered and user-led content creation and consumption that satisfies the CITL framework. The methodology, participants, and peer review process will be presented. The data and study findings will be analyzed through quantitative and qualitative methods. The findings, limitations, and recommendations will contribute to what is already known in the literature for the key design elements of quality VR-based experiences and where further study should focus on to enhance collaboration and creativity in the design of multimedia and multimodal learning settings in an ever-evolving immersive ecosystem.

## 2. Review of Related Work

Through the incorporation of peer feedback and an emphasis on user experience and reusability in the produsage context of today's digital media-informed societies, VR applications can be meticulously crafted to satisfy specific user requirements, culminating in more engaging and fulfilling experiences. In the context of the evaluation of Virtual Reality environments as learning and/or training environments, four rubric suggestions were identified. According to [ 24], media characteristics, presence, and flow were found to have significant impacts on learning efficacy [ 25, 26, 27, 28, 29]. They composed the evaluation areas and items in terms of the VR efficacy in use for training purposes as derived through literature analysis. Conducting a 2-round Delphi study, the final categorization includes three main areas, namely (1) Learning, (2) Media, and (3) Content quality. The items for area 1 (Learning) include (i) Activeness; (ii) Satisfaction; (iii) Flow; (iv) Interactivity; and (v) Learning effects. In Area 2 (Media), the items listed include: (i) Presence; (ii) Usability; (iii) Manipulability. In Area 3 (Content Quality), the items identified are (i) Content design; (ii) Teaching/learning strategies; and (iii) Learning contents. They suggest that the items of the tool would be selected depending on the type of content to be evaluated and can be utilized as a tool for assessing the contents' characteristics as a learning tool and for providing feedback.

Johnson-Glenberg's QUIVRR—Quality of Immersive VR in Education Rubric [ 30] provides a checklist for both scoring and qualitative feedback (evaluators' notes). The criteria are broken into three sections, namely, Pedagogy/Content, Mechanics, and bonus items that include assessment, adaptivity, and collaboration that were not included in the scoring system at that time. The author states that it may be more relevant for STEM content than Humanities. The design of the rubric takes into account the SAMR model [ 31] (Substitute, Augmentation, Modification, and Redefinition) and Kolb's (2017) [ 32] Triple E Framework for learning with technology (i.e., Engage, Enhance, and Extend). With respect to the SAMR model, the criteria check the transformative value in learning that technology offers, and more specifically, at the substitute level, the tool offers no functional change, making no difference from the old 2D technology; at the augmentation level, it makes a difference in the learning process; at the modification level, the technology allows for significant task redesign; and at the redefinition level, the technology allows for new tasks that were previously inconceivable. Regarding Kolb's [ 32] Triple E Framework, the criteria focus mainly on the scaffolding opportunities of the VR environment, seeking some “evidence of leveling up in difficulty with time or mastery” with the addition of components that enhance learning at an appropriate pace.

In 2021, Fegely and Cherner [ 33] created a comprehensive rubric for evaluating the quality of EduVR for mobile devices “as an addendum to extend Lee and Cherner's [ 34] foundational instructional app rubric [domains]” (p. 139). While [ 34]'s initial rubric domains focused on Instruction, Design, and Engagement (labeled as A, B, and C, respectively), ref. [ 33] included the domains of:

D. Positioning of the EduVR, with rubric dimensions of the use of EduVR (D1) and its educational impact (D2);

E. Avatar Level, with rubric dimensions of the avatar level (E1) and interactions (E2);

F. Virtual Environment, with rubric dimensions of authenticity and realism (F1), content representation (F2), navigational aids (F3), and multimedia elements (F4); and

G. Virtual Experience, with rubric dimensions on the experiential component (G1), pathways (G2), dimensionality of movement (G3), virtual manipulation (G4), specificity of control (G5), and immersion (G6).

All the abovementioned dimensions lie in the categories of virtual representation (E1, E2, F1, G6), learning theory (D1, D2), user experience (F2–F4), and human interactive design (G2–G5).

Examining the existing rubrics and quality criteria for the development and implementation of virtual experiences for learning and training purposes provides the foundation for a more critical selection of the criteria that will satisfy the needs of the present study. Literature suggests that utilizing metaverse-based learning environments may facilitate learner-centered teaching by promoting collaborative, problem-based learning while still making independent learning possible [ 35, 36, 37]. On the premise that VR platforms as Multi-user Virtual Environments (MUVEs) can foster collaboration, interaction, and creativity, these elements coupled with the soft skills enhancement that MUVEs facilitate [ 38, 39] will also be a new criterion to be added to the existing rubrics. Nevertheless, while VR offers numerous benefits for soft skills training, challenges such as cognitive overload [ 40] and distraction [ 41] may arise that must be addressed to optimize learning outcomes [ 42]. Additionally, as cost remains a challenge for the adoption of cutting-edge technologies in mainstream education, as [ 43] note, VR implementation scenarios should support the participation of users with high-end VR headsets as well as those using smartphones or computers, promoting inclusivity.

The QUIVRR criteria, especially items from the Pedagogy section, are congruent with the present study. Nonetheless, as the creators of the tool note, it was designed to assess the quality of a learning experience in a VR Head Mounted Display (HMD). As such, items from the Mechanics section, such as induction to cybersickness, eye strain, haptics, etc., cannot be adopted in our study that investigates the opportunities for inclusive, collaborative teaching and learning practices in Multi-user VR environments. Additionally, the current literature on immersive virtual soft skills training is limited, indicating a need for further research to validate its effectiveness [ 44, 45].

## 3. Proposed Rubric

The study of the related literature was a valuable guide to follow a scientific framework for the design and selection of the rubric criteria that support the needs of the present study. A framework of 19 design elements by [ 11] was taken into consideration coupled with [ 24], the eduVR and QUIVRR rubric criteria. We found a rich foundation to add our criteria or expand the existing ones, focusing mainly on Pedagogy, Instructional Design, Immersive Environments and Experiences, and the Overall VR prototype experience quality and Reusability. In order to maximize opportunities for reflection and self-reflection for improvements of the final version of the students' artifacts, the researchers agreed on the creation of a peer review form consisting of three parts. Part I consists of a rubric with 25 quality criteria (QC), with the addition of one extra criterion, all allowing for both score rating (1–5 scale) with a maximum score of 130 (25 + 1 criteria) and open comments on four categories as illustrated in [Table 1](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-t001) (Rubric Quality Criteria for VR Prototypes Peer Evaluation, Lazou et al., 2025, also available as [Supplementary Material](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#app1-information-16-00174)). 

**Table 1.** Rubric Quality Criteria (QC) for VR Prototypes Peer Evaluation 1 .

### 3.1. Part I—The Rubric Categories and Quality Criteria

Pedagogy (QC 1–10),

Instructional Design (QC 11–15),

Immersive Environment and Experience (QC 16–25),

Overall VR prototype experience quality/Reusability (as an extra criterion).

At the stage of categorization, we resonate with the creators of the existing rubrics' comments that there may be overlapping on which category some criteria may fall in. The criteria that were added to the existing rubrics were mainly QC 14 and QC 25 with a clear reference to the content overload* and distraction/disorientation* elements that bear reverse ratings in relation to the rest of the criteria due to their negative connotation but are essential for the findings related to the CITL framework. In addition, QC 19, QC 20, and QC 21, regarding collaboration and interaction*, sense of co-presence*, and soft skills practice opportunities*, were added as they are essential elements for the findings of the study. We understand that in the broader categorization of the existing criteria, these QC may have been implied, but they are not clearly stated. It is noteworthy that Johnson–Glenberg added the multi-user experience criterion as a bonus in the QUIVRR rubric with the note that it may be an additional basic element in the future. Nevertheless, we did not adopt the second part of her checklist, as Mechanics related to eye strain, induction of cybersickness, etc., are out of the scope of the present study.

The SAMR model related to the transformational power of the VR learning potential (QC 2, QC 11, and QC 15) and Kolb's Triple E framework on engagement, enhancement, and extension (QC 4) was also adopted. The additional element of reusability* (QC26) was employed as an extra QC to be evaluated. The reusability of VR elements is not seen as a mere decision to reuse content created by others, but under the lens of the produsage [ 18] and collective intelligence [ 19] content creation that emerges from the collaboration, collective efforts, and consensus decision-making in contemporary digital media-informed societies. Building communities of practice in innovative VR spaces additionally provides educational repositories of pilot-tested, peer-evaluated, and final versions of VR educational spaces, thus fostering innovation and continuous improvement through iterative refinement. As such, the peer evaluation and appraisal of quality work in the creation of specific learning objects in VR environments may lead to adaptations and modifications to suit other contexts, informing existing work and promoting overall creativity and quality of outputs.

### 3.2. Part II Comments and Things to Fix

Comments and Things to Fix consists of two open sections to complete that may be based on the rubric criteria but mainly urge the reviewers to exchange written feedback, namely (1) on the strengths of the VR prototype and (2) suggestions on what to fix. This section promotes constructivism and a Community of Inquiry (CoI) [ 46] with the participants sharing common interests in their professional development, within a framework of interdisciplinary studies, either at the early stages of their professional careers or not.

### 3.3. Part III Reflection from the Peer Review Process

Reflection—what I learned from the peer review consists of three open sections; namely; (1) about the peer review process; (2) about what I might change in my own VR prototype; and (3) about quality and reusability. This part aims at deep critical thinking and metacognition as essential elements of meaningful learning instances in the peer evaluation process.

This reflection was also shared with peers, while a more detailed report on the processes and knowledge, skills, and attitudes gained and changed was submitted as part of the final assignment in the form of a reflective report. [Table 1](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-t001) illustrates all three parts of the proposed rubric, including numeric and accompanying qualitative feedback in the form of notes.

## 4. Materials and Methods

For the needs of the study, we designed action research that involved participants selected from a postgraduate program with an interest in VR environments. The postgraduate program provides its students with an educational VR space, the Metaverse IMT VR space ( [https://imt.cs.duth.gr/metaverse.xhtml](https://imt.cs.duth.gr/metaverse.xhtml) (accessed on 21 January 2025)) [ 47], a VR ecosystem created on the Spatial.io platform, which consists of a multilevel virtual world with eight indoor spaces providing a variety of academic services and spaces for socializing and recreation [ 48, 49]. Among others, it was designed to provide lecture spaces, collaboration, and micro-teaching rooms to bring learners together, honing different skills and expertise, and urging them to work collaboratively with English as a medium of instruction [ 16] with the ultimate goal of creating their own VR artifacts. The IMT VR ecosystem provided the immersive space for the present study to be implemented based on its collaborative learning, communication, and interaction features. The stages of collaboration, as illustrated in [Figure 1](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-f001), involved the collaborative creation of the VR spaces that were added to the IMT VR ecosystem, the invitation to exchange microteaching sessions, the peer evaluation of the spaces, and the reflection process. 

**Figure 1.** The collaboration stages in the VR space.

The students' collaborative VR artifacts can be added as portals within the IMT VR space, creating a repository of good practices. This condition was important for the study as the students could leverage the space for gathering and entering the portals for the micro-teaching sessions, facilitating the process of peer evaluation during and after the sessions. ( [Figure 2](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-f002)). 

**Figure 2.** The Metaverse IMT Space and postgraduate students' collaborative VR spaces as portals.

Ethical and privacy considerations on the students' participation were clarified, proceeding only with students who accepted to sign the voluntary participation form in the study, as approved by the Research Ethics Committee of the University. Three explanatory sessions were provided by the researchers: the first one at the beginning of the course/study to explain the process and invite the students to decide on their participation in the study, the second one to explain the details regarding the VR platform and to form pairs/groups, and the third session (that succeeded their pairwork and artifact creation) to provide them with the rubric and clarify any queries about the micro-teaching sessions and the peer evaluation process. The other pair for the micro-teaching session and the peer evaluation was arbitrarily selected. There was some guidance on peer evaluation processes complying with the basic elements of constructive peer assessment as well as adult education principles in a social context while sharing diverse perspectives.

### 4.1. Participants

The study was conducted for two consecutive years (2022–2023 and 2023–2024) with 36 participants in an international postgraduate program course that provides training in building VR spaces for educational and training purposes. The participants come from different fields and are of different ages and past teaching experience. ### 4.2. The Stages of the Intervention

The participants were asked to design and create a VR space of their preference after forming pairs or groups of three. Upon developing their space, they were provided with the rubric with the quality criteria to cross-check their products with other pairs/groups in micro-teaching sessions. The final scores (numerical evaluation) and comments (qualitative feedback) were decided in pairs/groups and provided in written form which was emailed back to their peers. Parts II and III, as a reflection of the process, were also included. The instructors were copied in this email. The final stages included reflection on the process for all actors involved and final improvement of their VR environments based on their self-reflection and meta-cognition process ( [Figure 3](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-f003)).

All the research methods employed provided a rich dataset to cross-check the reliability of the study. Multiple data sources, the so-called triangulation, can verify the trustworthiness of research by establishing the values of (a) credibility, that is, how sure the researcher is about the truth of the study's findings; (b) transferability, that is, if findings can be applied to other contexts; (c) confirmability, that is, if findings are based on and portray the participants' responses—not on the researcher's bias; and (d) dependability, that is, if the findings would be consistent in case the study is conducted by other researchers [ 50]. Therefore, multiple data sources, the so-called triangulation, can verify the trustworthiness of qualitative research, which can sometimes be followed up with quantitative research. Yilmaz [ 51] states that “triangulation among complementary methods and data sources generally lead[s] to converging conclusions” (p. 319). Also, ref. [ 52] observes that qualitative research can further analyze and enrich quantitative data. Therefore, both methods are not to compete, but to be wisely selected so as to lead to new knowledge that reflects social reality.

The comments on each quality criterion, that is, the descriptive feedback that was offered to the designers and developers of the VR spaces, apart from the points attributed (scale 1–5), provided opportunities for reflection, new peer interaction within each team of creators, and actions within the VR environment to improve the design and assets of the educational setting. Regarding the last and extra criterion that refers to the overall quality of the VR prototype and its reusability, it is worth mentioning that in most cases it was observed that this part of the evaluation contained elements of the 25 QC that preceded. Strengths and weaknesses are highlighted with further recommendations for improvements.

### 5.2. Usefulness of the Peer Evaluation Process in VR Educational Spaces

With regard to research question 2, which investigates the usefulness of the peer evaluation process in VR educational spaces, Parts II and III of the rubric provided valuable data on the process, reflection, and decisions on the refinement of the VR spaces. In addition, the semi-structured interviews provided a more in-depth analysis, as illustrated in [Table 5](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-t005). More specifically, the categories that emerged focused on the usefulness of VR pilot testing, the peer review practices within VR settings, the importance of evaluation based on quality criteria, and the importance of leveraging VR technology for learning. 

**Table 5.** Categories and codes frequency table.

Following the grounded theory, codes and categories emerge from the data. Blodgett-Griffin [ 53] points out that “[i]nductive data analysis involves making sense of data” (Qualitative Data Analysis, p. 1). The author, in her [ 54] description of the three phases of coding in grounded theory, stresses that qualitative data analysis is not linear and, in each transcript, “codes and categories are arranged and rearranged to refine and reduce until a set of codes and categories that fits the data is established” (p. 1).

In the specific part of the study, the data were derived from the participants interviewed [ 55]. The three phases, namely, open, axial, and selective coding, were implemented. In the first phase, line-by-line open coding took place, with codes that were repeatedly present or consistently absent [ 56]. In this initial phase, as [ 57] suggests, coding stuck closely to the data (p. 47). In the second phase, the data had already been coded and their interrelationships were discovered. This phase allowed categories to emerge that in the third phase were revisited so as to identify core categories and subcategories that allowed a story to emerge. [Table 5](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-t005) thoroughly depicts the codes, categories, and frequencies of codes.

From the first category, namely the usefulness of VR pilot testing, the main elements that emerged were the 'purposeful practice' and the 'proactive approach', which, in alignment with the 'reflective thinking' and 'self-assessment opportunities', led to the identification of areas for refinement before the implementation in wide learning audiences. Additional codes that weave together with 'skills building' while being exposed to pilot teaching in VR spaces are other 'soft skills building' such as 'self-regulation' and 'time management'. These factors 'enhance confidence and preparedness', which correlates with the post-survey participant's responses regarding their readiness and intentions to adopt VR in their educational practices, as displayed in [Table 6](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-t006). 

**Table 6.** Participants' attitudes and perceptions after their peer-review process.

Regarding the second category of peer review practices in VR settings, the participants agree on the importance of 'constructive feedback', especially given the diverse demographics and exposure to 'different perspectives'. The interdisciplinary approach, prior teaching experience, and generation gap in either building or peer evaluating the spaces provided the peer review with rich 'diverse perspective' feedback from the lens of the VR user experience (UX), whose needs and preferences must be seen to and satisfied, thus responding to inclusive educational practices. This diversity led to a strong 'Community of Practice' that shares common interests and fosters 'positive reinforcement' through 'peer collaboration' and 'soft skills building'. The reflection on the peer review practices in VR settings yielded another important element, that of the need to pay 'immersive attention' to provide constructive feedback, which leads to the Critical Immersive-Triggered Literacy (CITL) framework.

According to the interviewees, the rubric for the evaluation of the MUVEs provided them with an 'instructional design tool' and a 'tool for reflection' with 'clearly structured' parts and 'clarity of criteria'. This clarity helps participants understand expectations and facilitates consistent 'comprehensive assessment', and 'objectivity in evaluation', with a focus and shared interest in 'targeted improvement'. Interestingly, the element of 'reusability' was of high frequency in the responses of the participants on the premise that they did not intend to merely reuse VR objects as displayed in their peers' spaces but adopt the contextualization of an idea or the conceptualization of a process that they found interesting, useful, and/or a good teaching and learning strategy while exploring and evaluating their peers' immersive environments.

The last category identified was the usefulness of VR technology as an 'advanced technology' whose usability facilitates 'innovation' in teaching through the 'disruptive and enhanced learning opportunities' it fosters. The participants additionally commented on the importance of the appropriate 'media selection' that provides not merely a substitute for another tool with no functional change but a redefinition of learning allowing for tasks that are not feasible in the real world. This way the learners develop new skills to critically interact with the media that satisfy their learning quest, thus developing Immersive Media Literacy, an element that falls under the umbrella of the CITL framework.

### 5.3. Challenges and Benefits of Peer Evaluation in VR Environments

Regarding research question 3, which investigates the challenges and benefits of peer evaluation in VR space creation leveraging the proposed rubric, the richness of data, both quantitative and qualitative, provided answers that contributed to the measurement of the participants' professional development and level of readiness to implement immersive learning in their teaching practices. As emerged from the coding of qualitative data, the elements of exchange of ideas and suggestions for refinement, different perspectives, and reusability of identified good practices in the peer evaluation process are highly beneficial for the improvement of the overall quality and effectiveness of the VR experience designed and developed while critically immersing themselves into learning instances.

Interviewees shared their reflections on the benefits of the peer review process with suggestions on improvements with statements like “our peers proposed an adjustment to the assessment method that we found useful to adopt”, …”[b]y receiving peer feedback, we were able to gain a deeper understanding of strengths and areas for improvement, as well as identify blind spots we may have overlooked”, or regarding the reusability aspect with statements like “[t]he idea of using a bubble to transition to another space was inspired by the layout our peers adopted in their own VR prototype”… or the different perspectives benefits of peer evaluation with reflection, like “[o]ur diverse educational backgrounds, with them being IT specialists and us being ESL teachers, were what united us”… “We were able to balance each other out and improve each other's VR world constructively…. For instance, our VR space was more educational-focused, while theirs resembled a game”…. “They opted for commenting on technical staff, such as the need for a map, a tutorial and proper signaling and the possibility of the space being cramped with content.”…“Given that our space was initially crafted to improve learners' receptive and productive skills in the English language, while educating them about…, we failed to notice some more technical and practical issues concerning easy and flexible navigation in the space”.

The benefits of peer evaluation via micro-teaching sessions were additionally commented on, based on the feedback received on a small, controlled scale of experimenting with the space and strategies to be employed before exposure to a larger audience. A participant commented that “[this] proactive approach allows instructors to develop appropriate solutions and strategies to overcome these challenges before a full-scale rollout”. The recruitment of peers to provide constructive feedback was seen as a process of sharing collective knowledge and experiences while striving for improvement. A participant noted that “[b]y embracing peer feedback, we can leverage the collective knowledge and experiences of our peers leading to improved performance”.

Challenges of the peer-review process were identified and addressed at a large scale, though not in all cases. The challenge of misinterpretation of the quality criteria of the rubric, especially in cases of reverse rating scales due to negative connotations such as QC14 and QC25, referring to content overload and distraction respectively, were addressed by clarifying the interpretations of scale 1 as highly likely to 5 as unlikely in contrast with 1 = low–5 = high for the rest of the criteria, as indicated as a footnote of the rubric ( [Table 1](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-t001), Part I). In addition, as stated in the Methodology section, there were three synchronous sessions offered to the participants of the study at various phases of the implementation process to ensure a clear understanding of all steps and address questions or misconceptions regarding the process of collaboration, as illustrated in [Figure 1](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-f001), or further explanations on the use of the rubric as a tool facilitating the process and ensuring objectivity.

Based on previous studies and on what literature on peer evaluation suggests [ 14, 16], the peer review process, which was neither anonymous nor a matter of courtesy to avoid indicating areas for improvement, set the assessment environment on sincere efforts to build collaborative, constructive feedback that involved the participants in tasks and activities that entailed positive interdependence on providing accurate and sincere feedback as an indispensable part of the successful refinement of the final product. This condition valued each member's contribution [ 58] towards achieving shared goals more effectively [ 4]. Another challenge regarding the access to the VR spaces to join for the peer review process was addressed by hosting all spaces within the IMT VR Reception Space with bubbles to travel to and back from each environment.

Unpredictable challenges reported were some technical glitches, connectivity, and latency issues that hindered user experience, or the design of VR experiences for a specific target group that required the assessors to step into others' shoes to view the space through the target group's eyes. Despite structured criteria, personal biases may influence evaluations, leading to inconsistencies in scoring among peers. The training sessions on how to use the rubric contributed to mitigating this issue. Nonetheless, there was a comment by an interviewee who noted that “[t]he most challenging part was the assessment, as it made us realize the challenges of grading and evaluating the work of others objectively and how important it is to be fair in order to help our classmates improve their work”. The 'objective grading' issue was addressed by the additional descriptive feedback option next to the numeric one, which the participants evaluated as an invaluable part of the peer review process.

Based on the post-intervention survey, [Table 6](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-t006) provides insights into participants' perceptions and attitudes on completion of all stages of the process. The participants responded to the seven questions listed, eliciting responses about their readiness to adopt immersive technologies into their teaching practices after the training sessions. As illustrated, out of the n = 36 participants, most answers on the Likert scale concentrate on the last two levels, namely, “very much”/”extremely”.

More specifically, as [Table 6](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/16/3/174#information-16-00174-t006) depicts, there are no responses at scale 1 = not at all, and as such the blue indicator of the legend does not appear in the figure body, while the overwhelming majority of responses lie at scales 4 = very much and 5 = extremely, with percentages that exceed 75% per question. Interestingly, 88.9% of the participants believe that based on their training and peer evaluation processes, the use of immersive technologies in their teaching practice will improve student engagement and learning outcomes.

## 6. Discussion

In an ever-evolving technology-enhanced educational landscape, we need to define the purposeful use of innovative technologies with criteria that ensure pedagogically sound solutions. In the context of virtual environments deployed for educational purposes, the literature suggests that there is a need for longitudinal studies [ 59] to develop a framework that will facilitate designers' and developers' readiness to adopt optimal solutions in the representations of reality and multisensory information. The present study explores the factors that can provide a framework for effective solutions to be adopted by professionals who aspire to provide rich immersive experiences for their target audiences. To this end, a two-year longitudinal study was conducted, recruiting participants from an international post-graduate program focusing on immersive technologies for educational and training purposes. In a VR collaborative learning environment, the participants exchanged micro-teaching sessions with the intent to provide numerical and qualitative feedback to their peers based on the proposed rubric with quality criteria. Though it yielded fruitful results and insights, the ultimate goal was not only the evaluation process per se but also the reflection on the quality of the VR spaces built and the refinement of the spaces before broader use with the target audiences.

On the premise that the related literature had to offer valuable insights to rely on and further expand to meet the needs of a collaborative, multi-user virtual environment as an educational context, there was a thorough investigation of the evaluation design principles that could inform all aspects of the study. The richness of the data that the methodology of this study brought to light provided a foundation for testing the effectiveness of the proposed rubric for the peer evaluation of multi-user VR design and development. More specifically, based on the research questions, the peer review process provided insights on (a) the key elements and features that VR experience designers should take into consideration for teaching and training in MUVEs; (b) the contribution of the peer evaluation process with specific evaluation criteria that help creators and reviewers to reflect on their practices and improve the design and development of VR educational spaces; (c) the participants' post-intervention perceptions and attitudes towards the adoption of MUVEs in their teaching practices; (d) implications for practice that will inform all education stakeholders of opportunities for future practices in education.

### 6.1. Key Elements of the Proposed Rubric as an Evaluation Framework for MUVEs Design

In the complex multimedia and multisensory nature of virtual environments, the proposed rubric can be leveraged either as a tool for instructional designers to guide the design of the intended learning experiences or as a peer evaluation tool that allows for the pilot testing of the prototype from the user's perspective based on a structured approach that has not been investigated in the existing literature. This led to the expansion of the existing tools reconsidering the design of MUVEs as an ecosystem where a new skillset can be developed, responding to the constantly emerging need for digital well-being, effective collaboration, empathy, sustainability, and civic engagement. In light of this, the relevant criteria added included interaction and collaboration; soft skills practice; co-presence; content overload; distraction and disorientation elements; and reusability. In the peer review process, literature [ 60] identifies the need for an expansion of the instructional design evaluation process with mixed methods that include both quantitative and qualitative criteria. The present proposed rubric, apart from the 26 criteria that provide numerical feedback and a corresponding section for comments, fills this gap with a structured reflection approach that Parts II and III indicate.

With the intent to fill the gap of developing a peer evaluation discourse opportunity, Part II purposefully focuses on the evaluators' commenting on their peers' (1) strengths of the VR prototype and (2) things to fix. This provides the 'space' and opportunity for richer feedback and 'peer discourse' for the prototype elements that must be appraised with the intent to be adopted for future implications or the elements that need to be amended, thus drawing attention for refinement. Part III consists of a reflection on the process that promotes the sustainability of the participatory culture and peer discourse among professionals [ 61] who share common interests and goals, thus creating a Community of Inquiry [ 46] in an immersive context that fosters insights into critical pedagogical and interdisciplinary collaborative learning design. This stage involves a reflection on (1) the peer review process; (2) what the reviewer might change in their own VR prototype; and (3) the quality and reusability of elements in the framework of the produsage [ 18], collective intelligence [ 19], and knowledge sharing in online platforms interaction among tertiary education students for the exchange of resources [ 62] for content creation. In contemporary digital media-informed societies, taking into consideration what the literature suggests, this proposed evaluation framework can lead to the advancement of metacognition in higher education VR instructional design training.

### 6.2. Peer Evaluation Contribution in Designing MUVEs as Educational Contexts

The peer evaluation process in higher education is an approach that has been acclaimed in the literature for offering considerable transferable learning, like teamwork, critical thinking, active engagement in the learning process, and construction of knowledge, while enhancing students' evaluative judgment. Unlike previous findings on preference for anonymity of peer evaluation for courtesy, to avoid conflicts, and encourage more critical evaluation [ 63, 64, 65], the present study findings revealed that the candid exchange of peer feedback when based on a well-structured rubric can be appreciated as a valuable proactive approach for training and learning. In the context of designing and developing MUVEs as educational settings, the implementation of the study based on the proposed rubric and subsequent framework as a tool for peer evaluation practices played a crucial role in the refinement of the VR spaces as it promoted peer discourse. The findings of the study revealed that the need to identify, assess, and comment on their peers' work based on each quality criterion while collaboratively exploring the VR space from the user's lens raised the need for critical attention in the immersive environment, namely, the element of 'immersive attention' as coded in the qualitative analysis of data. This condition validates the CITL framework [ 3] as the immersive media literacy framework that critically draws the learners' attention to the meaningful immersive-triggered learning instances that identify and discard unnecessary information that may be unintentionally disturbing the disruptive learning process.

The exposure to different perspectives and the need to justify personal choices and claims and/or adopt diverse perspectives was also an important finding in the contribution of the peer review process as a holistic learning process that, apart from the cognitive development, led to affective and social growth. In the higher education context, peer feedback is valued as a process-oriented pedagogical activity that facilitates the construction of students' academic knowledge and identity [ 66]. This multi-faceted professional growth opportunity identified the importance of pilot testing the prototypes as a purposeful practice and proactive approach that enhances confidence and preparedness in leveraging innovative and promising educational technologies that are still in their infancy on the broader implementation scale. While in the process, the findings revealed that participants additionally identified the need for self-regulation and time management in advanced technology-enhanced learning settings. As such, the microteaching sessions in the VR prototypes as an active peer review process created additional conditions for self-reflection and self-assessment while experimenting with peers.

### 6.3. Post Peer Review Perceptions and Attitudes Towards MUVEs Adoption in Education

The richness of the data of the study, both quantitative and qualitative, provided insights into the participants' perceptions and attitudes towards the adoption of multi-user virtual reality environments for educational purposes. More specifically, as emerged from the qualitative data, the participants were very satisfied with the peer review process based on the rubric's quality criteria that contributed to an evaluation framework of what ensures the quality of an immersive experience. The participants identified the purposeful suggestions for their VR prototype refinement that derived from different perspectives as an essential part of their professional development, which provided them with a deeper understanding of the strengths of their practices and what needs to be addressed. This constructive feedback for the refinement of innovative interventions, in the context of an instructional design towards a holistic approach to education and training, aligns with the trends of collective intelligence [ 19] and shared wisdom that promotes a well-informed instructional design and pedagogy for smart use of smart technologies.

The post-survey findings regarding the participants' satisfaction with the process and future implementation prospects based on the training they received for the design, development, and refinement of their VR environments for educational purposes converge with the qualitative findings. They attributed high scores to their post-intervention confidence to integrate immersive learning in their teaching from the pedagogical, technological, and instructional design perspectives. The reflection and metacognition stage of the micro-teaching and peer review process had a significant impact on their professional development. Internalizing the valued process of well-structured peer feedback and reflection and metacognition processes, the refinements they worked on in their VR environments led to their perceptions of the added value of the VR interventions in education at a percentage of 88.9 regarding their students' expected engagement and improvement of learning outcomes. The scores attributed to the high levels of the Likert scale, namely, “very much/extremely” for their confidence and readiness to adopt MUVEs in their teaching, are in alignment with their reflective comments and thus verify the triangulation of the findings, as these multiple data sources and complementary methods enhance the reliability and credibility of the study findings [ 51].

### 6.4. Implications for Practice

The findings of this study provide valuable insights for educators, instructional designers, and researchers working with MUVEs in education and training as well as policymakers. The structured peer evaluation rubric developed in this study supports the design of VR learning spaces that encourage collaboration, interaction, engagement, and immersive learning experiences, which are in alignment with the Critical Immersive-Triggered Literacy (CITL) framework [ 3] as an essential component for cognitive and metacognitive processes.

Peer evaluation plays a crucial role in improving instructional design. By using the proposed structured rubric, educators can refine their VR prototypes based on constructive feedback to improve them and better support their learning objectives as a proactive approach that addresses obstacles in the seamless implementation of innovative practices for engaging, user-friendly, and human-centered experience (UX) in VR settings, tailored to the users' needs.

The study also highlights the importance of soft skills such as communication, teamwork, and problem-solving. The collaborative nature of MUVEs provides opportunities for learners to engage in active discussions, peer reviews, and co-creation of content, preparing them for real-world professional interactions.

Digital well-being and usability concerns must be addressed when designing VR learning spaces. The rubric's inclusion of content overload, navigation ease, and immersive attention helps designers create environments that minimize cognitive overload and distractions while enhancing the overall learning experience.

Policymakers play a critical role in shaping the future of immersive learning by establishing frameworks for technology integration, quality assurance, and scalability. The study highlights the need for strategic investment in VR infrastructure, critical digital literacy programs, and professional development for educators to ensure sustainable adoption of immersive technologies. The structured peer evaluation rubric can serve as a benchmark for evaluating VR-based educational programs, providing clear, evidence-based criteria for assessing effectiveness.

### 6.5. Limitations and Considerations

The present study focuses on the pilot testing of a proposed rubric for MUVEs. To this end, there is a purposeful choice of the rubric key elements focused on pedagogical aspects for the instructional design of user-centered immersive experiences for their cognitive, affective, and social growth, while the mechanics and technical details that involve biometrics or physical and mental challenges such as eyestrain, nausea, etc. are not prioritized; neither is the use of expensive equipment.

The number of participants was limited and sparsely populated as the postgraduate program is international. This affordance provided opportunities for evaluation from different sociocultural contexts and attitudes but based on a rubric with common quality criteria. To enhance the validity and reliability of the findings, the study was conducted for two consecutive years, with 36 participants who actively participated in all stages of the research, though it was quite challenging to reach out to everyone due to the hectic pace of life of our times. Due to the collection of both numeric and descriptive data, the sample of the postgraduate students could not be very large per year, but the consistency in findings allows for generalizations and transferability on the usefulness of the proposed rubric and the peer evaluation process through micro-teaching sessions.

## 7. Conclusions

Multi-user Virtual Environments have been widely identified as a valuable tool to enhance opportunities for learning and training, among other uses in the Industry 5.0 era of hyperconnectivity and digitalized interaction and communication models [ 67]. Nevertheless, in order to effectively utilize the affordances that technology-enhanced learning has to offer, it is crucial that we design purposeful, learner-centered immersive experiences that promote the cognitive, affective, and social growth of the users. Pedagogical and instructional design considerations arise that need to be addressed by establishing a consistent framework that may inform the design and development of effective, rich in quality and experiences immersive environments. The present study focuses on the construction of a rubric with quality criteria that incorporate both numeric and descriptive evaluation among peers who share common interests in constructing valuable immersive experiences for teaching and training purposes through pilot testing of the environments under construction. The findings of the peer review process with the utilization of the proposed rubric revealed the usefulness of the tool as a pedagogical and instructional design companion and evaluator, which additionally provides opportunities for continuous refinement of the spaces under construction while promoting cognitive, affective, and social growth to the users. Future research directions may involve the incorporation of more specialized elements that apply to specific fields of training, which was out of the scope of the specific study, as well as a scoring scale and gradation of the feedback description for each quality criterion. Overall, the study makes a substantial contribution by offering a validated, user-informed rubric for VR evaluation, promoting reflective peer review practices, and validating new theoretical frameworks like CITL [ 3] in the context of Virtual Reality. It bridges gaps in instructional design and multi-user immersive learning, providing actionable insights for researchers, educators, and developers who are designing and assessing VR learning environments. The main points of the study's contribution to the literature could be summarized as follows:

**Contribution to the Literature**

A Comprehensive Rubric for Educational Multi-User VR Settings. The proposed rubric constitutes an evaluation tool with additional quality criteria tailored for VR learning in Multi-User Virtual Environments (MUVEs). The newly added criteria

QC14—content overload

QC19—interaction and collaboration

QC20—co-presence

QC21—soft skills practice

QC25—distraction/disorientation elements, and

QC26—reusability address gaps in existing evaluation tools by focusing on areas such as sociocultural belonging, digital well-being, collaboration, empathy, sustainability, and civic engagement, enriching the framework for designing and assessing immersive educational spaces and providing a structured approach to peer review in immersive environments, which has been underexplored in existing literature.

Insight into Critical Pedagogical and Interdisciplinary Collaborative Learning Design. The rubric's distinctive parts for numeric evaluation, descriptive feedback, and reflection provide actionable insights into critical pedagogical and instructional design elements for collaborative learning for cognitive, affective, and social growth, simulating modern interdisciplinary working spaces where individual contributions are valuable to the goal set. This offers a bridge between theory and practice in VR education by incorporating diverse perspectives, emphasizing the importance of interdisciplinary and inclusive educational practices, thus contributing to the understanding of community dynamics and the growing body of research on collaborative evaluation methods.

Peer Evaluation as a Reflective Practice. The study validates the impact of pilot testing of VR experiences among peers who share common interests, advancing review processes and models that foster innovation while ensuring alignment with user needs and learning objectives. This process highlights how reflective and constructive feedback and peer insights within VR settings improve the quality and effectiveness of learning environments. By integrating user constructive feedback, VR applications can be tailored to meet specific user requirements, resulting in more engaging and satisfying experiences, thus responding to the affective domain. In addition, while the creators are testing their environments in small-scale simulation sessions, they observe participants' non-verbal reactions, and with the rubric elements in mind, reflect and proceed to corrective steps, identified in this study as the proactive approach, before the large-scale implementation.

Iterative Design and Reusability: The findings reveal that peer feedback fosters iterative design as the prototype undergoes continuous refinement based on the peers' testing and evaluation process. In addition, the reusability element emerges not just as VR assets viewed as items to be adopted and/or replicated in other spaces, but as innovative ideas and conceptual frameworks, advancing the literature on sustainable educational design.

Soft Skills Development within VR Inquiry-Based Collaboration. The collaborative exploration of VR affordances for designing and building immersive experiences, in alignment with the phases of VR pilot testing and peer review and feedback process, fosters the development of soft skills such as self-regulation, time management, collaboration, empathy, expertise sharing, and collective effort input, which are increasingly recognized as essential in both education and the workplace, providing a new dimension to professional development practices in alignment with [ 68].

Addressing Challenges in VR Peer Review Contexts. The study acknowledges the importance of explanatory steps and support to address challenges such as biases, misinterpretations of criteria, and/or technical issues. Training sessions and descriptive feedback options were effectively implemented to mitigate these challenges, contributing to best practices in the design and facilitation of peer evaluation in VR. The emphasis on human-centered design prioritizing user needs and preferences aligns with emerging trends in VR user experience (UX) research, enhancing the usability and accessibility of VR educational tools.

Validation of the Framework of Critical Immersive-Triggered Literacy (CITL) which underscores the importance of immersive attention and immersive media literacy in the multimodal and multisensory VR environments is a theoretical addition that enriches the field by linking immersive learning to critical thinking, purposeful attention, and digital citizenship.

In conclusion, the present study outcomes of two-year action research that involved mixed methods informed the key evaluation criteria that proved to be critical for the design of quality immersive experiences, revealed the usefulness of the rubric created to facilitate the pilot testing of the prototypes of VR instructional designers, and challenges and benefits that arise from peer evaluation practices in the context of interdisciplinary, diverse age, and professional experience demographics. Digital, content, and pedagogic concerns that arise among tertiary education peers in collaborative, produsage practices in the era of collective intelligence and wisdom can provide a fruitful field for the expert exchange of ideas for the refinement of the design of their VR environments.
