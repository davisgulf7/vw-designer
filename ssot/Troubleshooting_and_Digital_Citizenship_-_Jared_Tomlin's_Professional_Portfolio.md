## Troubleshooting and Digital Citizenship

A two-paragraph overview of troubleshooting and digital citizenship. Topics of this overview are:

Troubleshooting is the process of resolving issues with the technologies and technological resources implemented in K-12 classrooms.

Even with the best planning and preparation, there is always the potential for technological issues to arise during lessons. These issues can range from monitors and displays not working to systems not being able to connect to the internet to problems with external hardware such as the printer or sound system. This is why it is critical for educators to know how to troubleshoot and resolve these issues if and when they arise.

Throughout this semester, I have learned how to utilize different technologies and formats in my lessons. I have become more familiar with audio and video file formats as well as how to help students resolve potential technical issues through troubleshooting. In addition, I have learned how to create lesson where students can use technology in a way that addresses their higher order thinking according to Bloom's Taxonomy as well as higher LoTi levels.

There are a number of valid concerns that administrators, teachers, and parents might have about technology use in the classroom. Some examples of potential issues for concern include internet safety, student privacy, and the use of copyrighted materials. Fortunately, as previously discussed in other pages (see Online Projects), there are different ways to resolve these potential issues. These include the use of pseudonyms in publishing students' work to protect their anonymity, teacher-review of resources and technological materials provided to students, and awareness/adherence to policies regarding the use of copyrighted materials.

For this portfolio, viewers will see five potential issues that teachers and students may face in the classroom as well as troubleshooting strategies and links to resources in order to resolve said issues.

Monitor/Display Screen

One of the most common potential technology issues

involve the monitor or display screen not coming on. This trouble is caused by either a lack of power to the computer/device or lack of connection between the monitor and the rest of the system. To resolve this, first turn on the monitor. If the screen does not come on, check to make sure the computer/device is turned on. For computers with an external monitor, check to make sure the monitor is properly connected to both the computer and the power supply. I will use this strategy in my classroom to quickly help students resolve monitor/display issues when utilizing computers/devices during lessons or classwork completion.

[Jessica Gehman - Troubleshooting Technology in the Classroom - YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=c590whs93sg&t=17s)

Sound System

Another potential trouble that can arise is sound system malfunctions. When this trouble arises, it can either be lack of sound, feedback, static, or the sound system going to sleep while playing audio from the computer. Fortunately for each of these potential scenarios, there is a troubleshooting strategy to resolve it. For lack of sound, check the volume/audio settings on the computer/device, make sure to audio receiver is powered on, and check the audio cable connection. For feedback, check the microphone volume, and make sure that the teacher and student mics are on different channels. For static or dropout, f ully charge or replace the microphone battery, and ensure that the line of sight to receiver or ceiling sensor is working by checking that the ceiling sensor light is on. For the sound system going to sleep, t urn computer volume to 100% on the volume/audio settings, and adjust volume on the audio receiver. I plan to use this troubleshooting strategy in my lesson presentations when potential sound system problems may arise.

[Troubleshooting Classroom Technology (graniteschools.org)](https://www.graniteschools.org/edtech/tip/basic/troubleshooting/)

Projector

Another potential trouble that can arise is with using overhead projectors and document cameras. When this trouble arises, there are a number of potential causes such as the projector not turning on or turning on but not projecting. Each of these scenarios has a different troubleshooting strategy to resolve it. If the projector does not turn on, check to see if the batteries are dead, the projector is unplugged, or if the projector lamp is dead. In the latter case, a "replace lamp" light will come on and may require additional help via a work order to clean the projector filter. If the projector comes on but nothing is displayed, check the connection of the display cables, search all inputs using the search button on the projector remote, and use the blank button to make sure the projector is not set to blank. I plan to use this troubleshooting strategy in my classroom when a projector issue arises during lesson presentations.

[Troubleshooting Classroom Technology (graniteschools.org)](https://www.graniteschools.org/edtech/tip/basic/troubleshooting/)

Printer

Troubles can often arise with external hardware like printers. These issues can range from not powering on to lack of wireless connection. For troubleshooting, first make sure that the printer is turned on by checking that the power is turned on and the display comes on. Check the connector cable back to the surge protector. If the problem is connectivity, check the wireless connection; the display will often indicate in there is a connectivity problem. This case can often be resolved by checking the connection of the wireless cable or by troubleshooting the connection on the printer display (see below for more details). I plan to use this strategy in my classroom when I need to print worksheets or materials for students during class or if students need to use the printer for their assignments.

[Printer Troubleshooting - YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cIcrJQgx2KA)

Wi-Fi Router/Connectivity Issues

Not all troubles arise with hardware. In some cases, software issues can arise. One example of this issue is with the Wi-Fi or wireless connection. Most classrooms have some form of wireless router with the Cisco Radio being one of the most common. Although problems with connectivity are rare for teachers to deal with, such problems can be potentially resolved by checking the lights on the router. Almost every router has lights to indicate potential connectivity issues. For the Cisco Radio, solid blue and green lights indicate normal operation; blinking dark blue lights indicate software updates, solid amber lights mean IOS errors, and blinking red lights mean boot loader errors. Most routers utilize a similar system. For troubleshooting, check the lights and respond accordingly. Some routers allow the user to refresh the signal by pressing a button which can often resolve connectivity problems. Otherwise, the teacher may have to contact maintenance personnel and utilize alternative teaching methods. I plan to use this strategy when presenting my lessons or having students work with technology to complete assignments.

[Troubleshooting Classroom Technology (graniteschools.org)](https://www.graniteschools.org/edtech/tip/basic/troubleshooting/)

Digital Citizenship

Digital citizenship the process to prepare students for global technology demands of the future and to gain the ability to engage with the internet and technology in a safe and meaningful way. That being said, online safety is a major aspect of this process.

Developing safe and strong passwords is very important. This means:

Don't share passwords

Passwords cannot be anything personal

Be sure to include special symbols

Longer passwords are better

Student Agency: Student ownership, managing projects, leadership

Digital citizenship can be divided into nine elements:

Literacy (Understanding the right way to utilize technology/right technology for use)

New technologies

Commerce (Understanding use of money through online methods)

R ecognize dangers

Communication (Ability to use different online tools to communicate)

Apps, cellphones, code of behavior

Access ("Digital Divide"; equality in internet access)

Socioeconomic factors

Etiquette (Exhibiting appropriate behavior while online)

Law (Responsibility to follow legal guidelines)

NO sending viruses, actions against cyberbullying, hacking, plagiarism

Digital Rights (Rights and freedoms extended to digital world)

Constitution, free speech, privacy, and responsibility

Health (Improper use of technology can be detrimental to one's health)

Stress, headaches, eye strain, contact disconnect

Security (Taking proper steps to protect oneself from dangers)

Avoiding malware/viruses, backing up data, locking devices

It is important to teach digital citizenship to K-12 students. There are two pillars to this:

Respect (Etiquette, Acceptable Use Policy, Decency)

Protection (Identity Theft, Dark Web, Catfishing)

I have learned about Digital Footprint (Personal information someone leaves behind intentionally or unintentionally including web pages we visit). I have also learned about the multiple aspects of digital citizenship as well as why it is so critical to instill digital citizenship in students.

There are a number of concerns that administrators, teachers, and parent might have about digital citizenship in K-12 classrooms. These concerns include digital etiquette, internet safety, and student privacy. Fortunately, these concerns can be addresses through review of internet resources provided to students, careful and monitored use of information online, and the instillation of respect and adherence to online policies.
